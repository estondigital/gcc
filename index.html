Root Cause Analysis

You don't have a traffic problem.
You have a clarity problem.

Most B2B SaaS companies invest in more marketing without fixing the underlying system. Here's what's actually happening inside your GTM.

01
Weak Positioning
You look like everyone else. Buyers can't understand why you specifically. So they don't buy — or they buy the competitor who explains it better.
02
Generic Messaging
Your copy talks about features, not outcomes. It turns visitors into confused prospects who ghost your sales team mid-funnel.
03
Rising CAC
You're paying more to acquire customers who churn faster. This is a structural ICP problem, not a channel problem — and it compounds.
04
Misaligned GTM
Sales and marketing are playing different games. Leads don't convert because the narrative breaks down at exactly the wrong moment.
05
No Growth System
You're running one-off campaigns hoping something sticks. Revenue requires a repeatable, engineered system — not a calendar of tactics.
06
Wrong Demand Motion
You're generating awareness but not intent. Buyers exist — they just don't understand why now, why you, or why they should act at all.

Contrarian Thinking

What 9 years of B2B SaaS
actually teaches you.

Most growth advice is optimized for shares, not revenue. Here's what I actually believe.

More leads ≠ more revenue.
The fastest path to growth is almost never more top-of-funnel. It's fixing the leaks in the funnel you already have. Most companies are sitting on 2–3× revenue potential in existing pipeline — they just can't see it because they're too focused on acquisition.
CAC problems are messaging problems.
When your CAC keeps rising, the instinct is to test new channels. But 80% of the time, the root cause is that your messaging doesn't create self-service demand. Fix the message, and every channel gets more efficient simultaneously.
Growth is a system, not a tactic.
Companies that achieve durable growth aren't running better campaigns. They've built a system where positioning, demand generation, and sales enablement compound together. Individual tactics deteriorate. Systems improve over time.
